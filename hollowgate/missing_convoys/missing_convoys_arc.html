Surface Problem

Town Situation
  • Multiple supply convoys missing on roads near Hollowgate
  • Food variety disappearing, prices rising
  • Travelers nervous, parents warning children from the woods
  • Mayor frustrated and overwhelmed
  • Rumors spreading faster than facts
Intended Tone

Start grounded: hungry townsfolk, frustrated merchants, missing wagons. Then slowly shift into "something about this is wrong."

This is the party's first "larger world" mystery. It should feel solvable at first — then reveal it never was just a kobold problem.

What Actually Happened

The Truth

A mysterious human agent hired kobolds to ambush supply convoys. The kobolds were not interested in the food — they were instructed to retrieve a specific crate from one convoy.

  • The crate contains a strange old book tied to the deeper corruption storyline
  • The kobolds took the other wagons mostly because they could
  • The employer is now dying in the kobold den after being mauled by the kobolds' "pet"
This convoy theft intersects directly with Eric Huwithakay's investigation.
The Alarming Detail

Kobolds don't normally do this. They scavenge, steal livestock, raid isolated farms. Coordinated convoy ambushes strongly suggest outside influence — which is exactly what should bother the mayor and Eric.

The Tavern

Food Situation

Meals increasingly limited: potatoes, salted vegetables, watered ale, stale bread. No fresh meat. No luxury ingredients. The tavern keeper allows the convoy driver to stay for free.

"If the food wagons stop coming, we all suffer."
Gossip
  • Farmers report strange noises and movement near fields at night
  • Parents increasingly warning children away from the forest
  • Sounds like normal danger gossip — but subtly reinforces the roads are becoming unsafe

Wagon Investigation

The Food Was Not Taken

When the party finds the abandoned wagons, the supplies remain: sacks of potatoes, barrels of ale, grain, preserved food. This should feel wrong. Bandits steal valuables. Hungry kobolds should steal food. They didn't. They wanted something else.

Kobolds of Aelanthar
Appearance · Movement · Culture · What makes them alarming
▼
Physiology

Small reptilian humanoids with avian traits suggesting ancient evolutionary divergence rather than draconic ancestry. 3½–4½ feet tall, upright and alert, with disproportionately powerful hind legs built for sudden bursts of speed, climbing, and leaping.

  • Narrow torsos, long clawed fingers, thick digitigrade legs, heavy tails for balance
  • Scales: deep green, mossy olive, bruised purple, muddy teal, dark earthen tones
  • Bright feather plumes on head, spine, elbows, or tail — orange, turquoise, crimson, yellow, white
  • Plumes serve social signaling, intimidation, attraction, and status display
Movement & Presence

These kobolds move differently than goblins. They bob slightly while standing, make rapid head movements, chirp and hiss while speaking, leap sideways unnaturally fast.

  • Heavy hind legs let them vault onto wagons, spring over obstacles, scramble up cave walls
  • When startled: instinctively flare neck feathers
  • When threatened: stomp, hiss, chatter, rapidly click teeth
Status & Decoration

Higher-status kobolds exaggerate their plumes heavily with dyed feathers, beads, bits of metal, fake jewelry, and scavenged decorations. The chieftain's "royal regalia" is essentially molted feathers, broken necklaces, polished trash, and fake gemstones — arranged with absolute sincerity.

Cultural Behavior — Why the Attacks Are Alarming

These kobolds are not naturally organized raiders. Normally they scavenge, trap small game, steal livestock, raid isolated farms, and avoid heavily armed targets.

The fact they coordinated convoy ambushes strongly suggests outside influence. Which is exactly what should bother the mayor and Eric.

Someone organized them. That someone is now dying in their den.

Robyn
Kobold Scout / Outcast
Robyn doesn't fit in with the other kobolds. He's smaller, not as fierce, and gets picked on for it. He'd rather hide than fight. He speaks in a nervous, stammering tone — quickly, apologetically, and with the cadence of someone who expects to be interrupted at any moment.
Appearance
Physique. Smaller than other kobolds, with softer coloration and drooping, uneven feather plume. Faded colors. One feather appears badly dyed.
Posture. Slouches and avoids eye contact. Tail constantly twitching. Constantly fidgets. Flinches at loud sounds.
First Impression. He should look like a kobold who never successfully became "properly kobold" by the standards of his tribe.
Personality
Voice. Nervous, stammering, apologetic. Speaks quickly when frightened. Talks over himself. Bad at intimidation.
Motivation. Just wants to be left alone. Does not want to hurt anyone. Was coerced into participating — threatened with loss of tail feathers if he refused.
"I-I didn't wanna come with 'em... They said if I didn't, they'd... they'd tear off my tail feathers... I-I just wanted to stay in the nest..."
What Robyn Reveals
The Crate. The kobolds were told to retrieve an "important crate." "Boss said special. Not food crate."
The Employer. A human hired them. The kobolds attacked convoys and stole wagons, but the crate was the primary objective.
The Caverns. Robyn can lead the party toward the kobold caverns — if they make him feel safe enough to do so.
GM Notes — Running Robyn
  • Robyn should feel oddly sympathetic. Not heroic. Just not built for kobold life.
  • He responds much better to patience and kindness than intimidation — use his fear of the other kobolds as leverage if needed.
  • If the party treats him well, he becomes a useful recurring contact who can provide information about kobold movements and tunnel layouts.
  • He genuinely doesn't know what was in the crate — only that it mattered to "the boss."

Krobb
Kobold Guard · Entry Post · Trap Architect
Krobb is very proud of his traps. He designed them himself. He drew a map. He is certain that no one has ever, or could ever, get through them without that map. He is asleep when the party arrives. The pit trap is visible from the doorway. So is the cheese.
Appearance
Physique. Average kobold build, slightly soft around the middle. Wears a helmet slightly too large for his head. A sign on the wall behind him reads GUARD DUTY = VERY IMPORTANT! (PROBABLY).
Posture. Slumped in his chair, mouth open, snoring with the confidence of someone who has never once been ambushed.
Personality
Self-Assurance. Absolute. Krobb is not stupid enough to know he is not smart. He believes his trap system is the finest in the tunnels, possibly in the world. He has explained this to several kobolds who have stopped listening.
Bravery. He has none, but he believes he has enormous amounts of it. When genuinely threatened, this illusion evaporates instantly and completely.
His Spear. He has one. He does not know where it is. He will look for it if threatened. He will not find it in time.
"Traps are... very good traps. Cannot pass without map. I make them. Very hard. Very clever. You would not get past."
"Wait — where is spear? I had spear. Spear was HERE."
If Questioned
Cooperation. Immediate and total. Krobb has no loyalty strong enough to survive eye contact with someone who means business. He will explain every trap in loving detail, warn about the pit, confirm the cheese is not free, and describe the cavehopper's location with the hushed voice of someone who has had a very bad week because of it.
What He Knows. Trap locations, the jail area layout, that the cavehopper is behind the rocks, that the two guards at the jail hate their assignment, and that the chieftain has "important guests" (the party). He does not know what the crate contains.
GM Notes — Running Krobb
  • The comedy is in his complete sincerity. He is not joking about the traps. He genuinely believes they are formidable. Play his pride straight.
  • When the party points out the pit is visible, he will explain that this is actually part of the trap — a distraction. It is not a distraction. It is just a visible pit.
  • His missing spear is somewhere in the room. The GM can decide where. It should not be anywhere useful.
  • If the party treats him with any decency at all, he becomes oddly grateful and surprisingly forthcoming. He has been assigned to this post for a long time and is lonely.
  • He is not present at the den during the chieftain scene. He is at his post. Asleep.

The Chieftain
Kobold Chieftain · Self-Declared King
Unexpectedly charismatic. Deep, rich, textured voice. Speaks like he believes he is a great king — because, by his own reckoning, he is. His "royal regalia" is essentially molted feathers, broken necklaces, polished trash, and fake gemstones, all arranged with absolute sincerity. He is simultaneously ridiculous and oddly compelling.
Appearance
Regalia. Huge feather crest — dyed, layered, magnificent by kobold standards. Gold-painted stones. Dangling trinkets. Layered shiny junk. Fake gemstones. Exaggerated posture.
Den. His throne room is absurdly regal by kobold standards: fake jewelry, painted stones, cracked goblets, glitter-covered junk. He believes it is magnificent.
Personality
Voice. Deep, deliberate, theatrical. He savors his own words.
Pride. Deeply offended that the crate contained only a book. He considers this a personal insult from the dying human.
The Show. Rather than threatening the party, he proudly presents the cavehopper as his greatest treasure. He wants admiration. He is genuinely delighted by how cute it is. He does not anticipate what happens next — or if he does, he considers it the party's fault for not being still enough.
"Little man hired us. Wanted shiny box. We took extra wagons too. Seemed fair."
"Look. LOOK. Is beautiful, yes?"
After the Cavehopper Triggers
Reaction. Annoyed — not at the party, but at the situation. He wanted a demonstration, not a mess. He waves them out through a back tunnel with evident impatience.
The Chase. The kobolds who follow are herding, not hunting. Rocks and spears are thrown more out of embarrassment than genuine aggression. The chieftain stays behind.
GM Notes — Running the Chieftain
  • Play him as genuinely believing in his own grandeur — never winking at the camera. The comedy comes from his absolute sincerity.
  • He is not a villain. He took a job, is annoyed the crate was worthless, and mostly wants to show off his pet to these interesting strangers.
  • The cavehopper presentation should feel like a proud parent showing off a child. He is beaming. His court nods approvingly. It is genuinely a good moment — until it isn't.
  • He does not intervene when the cavehopper triggers. He watches. Whether he's amused, resigned, or disappointed in the party's poor stillness is up to the GM.
  • After the cavehopper fight, his annoyance is the annoyance of someone whose party trick went sideways. He wants the mess gone, not revenge.
  • His voice should feel incongruously deep and regal for a 4-foot reptile wearing glitter.

The Dying Human
Unknown Agent · Elkenquay-Associated
A human lies mauled and barely alive in the kobold den. He wears colors associated with Elkenquay. Most parties will not recognize this immediately. The kobolds' pet attacked him. The chieftain seems unconcerned.
Condition
State. Mauled, barely conscious, unlikely to survive without magical healing. If the party stabilizes him, he can provide fragments of information before slipping unconscious again.
Markings. Wears colors associated with Elkenquay. A DC 14 check identifies markings tied to Elkenquay. Characters with noble, military, merchant, diplomatic, or espionage backgrounds may recognize them automatically.
His Possessions
Coinage. Various Elkenquay currency.
Documents. Partially burned correspondence, coded ledger fragments, tariff records, convoy route maps, seal fragments — references to "adjusted taxation" and "diverted cargo."
Nothing explicitly identifies a grand conspiracy yet. It should feel suspicious, incomplete, and political.
GM Notes
  • This man connects the kobold attacks to the regional conspiracy — but that connection should feel circumstantial, not explicit, on first discovery.
  • If the party tries to question him, he can provide fragments: he was sent to retrieve the crate, he doesn't know exactly what's inside, he was told it was important to "certain arrangements."
  • Whether he lives or dies is up to the party — and their choice has consequences for what they can learn.
  • His Elkenquay markings are the same thread Aurelia's briefing hints at from the other direction.
✦
Eric Huwithakay
Gate Scribe · Sarynthian Intelligence Contact · Found Dead
Eric was not a gate scribe. He was a Sarynthian intelligence operative embedded in Hollowgate, tracking unusual tariffs, altered manifests, and missing cargo. He got close to the truth. The party finds him in the kobold tunnels — already dead, satchel still on his body. He never filed his final report.
Discovery
When Found. While escaping the caverns, the party comes across his body in the tunnels. This should land with weight. He was here for the same reasons they are. He just arrived first.
Cause of Death. Not immediately clear. Not kobold weapons. The party may or may not connect this to the cavehopper on their own.
His Satchel
Bronze Signet Stamp. An official-looking seal — Sarynthian, though most party members won't recognize it immediately. Someone will.
Partial Tunnel Map. Hand-drawn, more detailed than Krobb's. He'd been down here before.
Final Note. Three words in a tight, careful hand: "Kobolds. Someone is paying them." That's all he had time to write.
"Kobolds. Someone is paying them."
What His Presence Means
The Arc Shifts. Finding Eric is the moment the convoy problem stops being a local nuisance and becomes something larger. He was sent here by someone who already suspected what the party is only beginning to piece together.
Princess Aurelia. Knows Eric was Sarynthia's contact in Hollowgate. His correspondence stopped. She's been waiting for news that hasn't come. The party now carries it.
GM Notes
  • Eric doesn't need to be a character the party ever spoke to. His weight comes entirely from what he represents: someone who knew, tried to act, and didn't make it out.
  • The final note — three words — should feel like the most important thing in the tunnels. Play the moment quietly. Let the party sit with it.
  • His signet stamp connects to Aurelia's storyline. If she's in play, she will recognize it immediately. If not, it's a thread for later.
  • His presence confirms the convoy theft was never just a kobold problem — someone organized this, and Eric knew it.
  • Do not over-explain. Let the satchel speak. The party will ask the right questions.

The Guard Post

Krobb — Genuinely Asleep

A single kobold guard sits slumped at a crude wooden post just inside the cavern entrance. He is not pretending. He is not a trick. He is asleep — deeply, completely, with the total commitment of someone who takes guard duty very seriously in theory and not at all in practice.

On the desk in front of him: his map, face-up. On his belt: two stoppered vials. Immediately visible from the entrance: a large block of cheese sitting next to an open bear trap. Also immediately visible, directly behind his desk: a pit in the floor.

The party can see two of the five traps without moving. Krobb is certain this tunnel is impenetrable.
What the Party Can Do
  • Take the map and potions silently — DC 12 Stealth. On failure, Krobb stirs but doesn't fully wake. A second failure wakes him.
  • Let him sleep and pass — safest. He won't know they came through.
  • Wake and question him — terrified, stammering, immediately cooperative. Will explain the traps in great detail and with great pride. Will not fight. Will look for his spear. Will not find it.
  • Intimidate — works instantly. He crumbles. Still cannot locate his spear.

Loot — On Krobb's Person

Items
  • The trap map
  • Two minor healing potions (2d4+2 hp each)
  • 3 copper pieces and a very shiny button he considers extremely valuable

Navigating the Traps

Using Krobb's Map

If the party has Krobb's trap map, they can reference it against what they encounter — each trap roughly matches the map's crude diagrams. Players who engage with the map should feel rewarded: advantage on Perception checks to spot traps, and they know what type of hazard to expect before they see it.

If Krobb was questioned and cooperative, he'll have told them which side of the tunnel to hug for the Slippy Spot and that the Noisy Thing strings are at chest height — worth communicating.

The map doesn't show everything. It shows what Krobb thought was worth drawing.

The Dark Corridor

Atmosphere — Before the Guards

The tunnel here changes character. The crude torchlight of the entry gives way to near-darkness — a deliberate choice. The ceiling drops lower. The air is colder, damper, heavier. Sounds carry differently: the distant drip of water, something settling in the rock, the faint murmur of voices ahead.

This section is meant to feel like something is being kept here. Not imprisoned. Contained.

The Two Guards

Not Alert — Not Dangerous

Two kobolds stand (one is actually sitting) near a crude barricade of stacked stones and old timber. They are talking. Not quietly. They have been here long enough to be bored, and bored kobolds are chatty kobolds. They are not watching the corridor behind them.

They are not the chieftain's best. These are the kobolds who lost the assignment lottery.

The Jail Area

First Impression — Appears Empty

Beyond the guards, a crude jail: rough stone walls, a few rusted bars wedged into the rock, a floor of loose gravel. It looks unused. No prisoners visible. No recent signs of occupation.

The guards don't go in. They don't look at the far end.

The Hidden Section — The Cavehopper

The far wall of the jail is dark — truly dark, beyond what torchlight reaches. A deliberate arrangement of rocks and rubble creates a visual barrier, not a physical one. Behind it: the chieftain's pet. The cavehopper is being kept here between showings.

This is why the guards don't look at it too long. This is why there are no keys — the rocks are the containment. This is why neither guard would rather die than run.

The party may encounter the cavehopper here — cute, twitchy, enormous black eyes — before the chieftain proudly presents it in the den. Same trigger rules apply.
Searching the Jail
  • DC 12 Investigation: scratch marks on the floor and walls of the alcove — deep, frantic, inconsistent. Whatever made them moves fast and in multiple directions
  • DC 14 Perception: faint sounds from behind the rocks — soft chirping, small shifting movements. Not obviously threatening
  • The rocks are not locked or secured — they can simply be moved
If the Party Releases It Early

The cavehopper, if freed here, does not immediately attack the party — provided no one moves suddenly. It investigates them with its nose. It cocks its head. It makes a small chirping sound.

If it remains calm, it may trail the party deeper into the tunnels. It will attack any kobold it encounters. It will also attack the party the moment someone triggers it.

Releasing it is not safe. It is also not without tactical value. The chieftain will be furious.

Guard Behavior — Flee Over Fight

Their First Instinct Is Away

These guards are not brave. They are not loyal. When surprised or threatened, their overwhelming instinct is to run — not toward the exit, but deeper into the tunnels, toward the chieftain's den. This is not tactical. This is kobold panic.

If one flees, the other almost certainly follows. They will shout the whole way.

Their running is the party's timer. The chieftain will know something is wrong before the party arrives.
Loot — If the Party Searches the Bodies
  • Two crude kobold daggers (1d4 piercing, functional)
  • No keys. Neither guard carries keys to the jail — or anything else of note
  • One has a piece of dried meat he was clearly saving
No keys means the party's only way in is moving the rocks — which raises the question of whether moving rocks makes noise.

  • Low ceilings — full-sized humanoids must duck

The Caged Creature

Something Is Being Kept In

The party passes a guarded section where the guards seem more concerned with keeping something in than keeping intruders out. Behind bars and around a bend: scratching, soft growling, tiny squeaking noises. The creature is not fully visible.

If released early, it may later aid the party — or at minimum attack kobolds indiscriminately.

The Den

Contents
  • Roughly 12 kobolds + the chieftain + the dying human
  • Absurdly regal by kobold standards: fake jewelry, painted stones, cracked goblets, glitter-covered junk
  • The crate is here — opened and visibly disappointing

The Pet — The Reveal

The Chieftain Shows Off

Rather than threatening the party, the chieftain proudly presents his pet. He is showing off. This is his prize possession — his greatest treasure. He wants the party to admire it.

Initially: bunny-like, twitchy, enormous black eyes. Almost unbearably cute. The chieftain beams. His court murmurs approvingly.

The cavehopper investigates the party with its nose. It cocks its head. It makes a small chirping sound.

Then someone moves. Or reaches for a weapon. Or takes a step. Or breathes wrong.
The Trigger

Any sudden movement — standing up, drawing a weapon, stepping forward, reaching out to touch it — causes the cavehopper to snap. Jaws split too wide. Posture drops into a predatory crouch. Eyes go flat and predatory. It moves with violent, unnatural speed.

The chieftain does not intervene. He watches with the expression of someone watching their prize horse perform.

This is the party's first strong "something unnatural is happening" moment.
The Chase

After the fight, the chieftain is annoyed — not at the party, but at the situation. He wanted a demonstration, not a mess. He waves them out impatiently through a back tunnel.

The kobolds chase them with rocks and spears more out of embarrassment than genuine pursuit. They are herding, not hunting.

"Out. OUT. You ruin everything."

The Crate

Contents

The kobolds are disappointed. The crate contains only an old book, wrapped carefully — non-metal, non-shiny. The chieftain finds this deeply insulting. He considers the human employer a fraud.

The book itself is tied to the deeper corruption storyline. Its significance is not immediately obvious — it looks old, carefully preserved, and not obviously valuable.

What the book actually is remains for the GM to determine. The party should recognize it matters without knowing why.

Escaping the Caverns

The Chase

After the pet fight, the chieftain forces the party out through a different tunnel route. Soon: kobolds begin chasing them, shouting, throwing rocks and spears. The exit is disorienting and the kobolds know the tunnels far better than the party.

Eric's Body — The Transition Moment

Found While Escaping

While escaping the caverns, the party discovers Eric Huwithakay's corpse. This should land heavily. He was investigating this, getting close to answers, and likely silenced.

His satchel is on him — containing the partial tunnel map, the final note about kobolds, and the bronze signet stamp.

This is the major transition from "kobold problem" to "larger conspiracy."
What His Presence Confirms
  • Eric traced the missing convoys to exactly this location
  • He entered the tunnels to gather proof before reporting
  • He did not survive what he found
  • His investigation and the party's are now the same investigation

The dying human's Elkenquay markings connect this arc to the regional political conspiracy driving tensions between Elkenquay and Fastingbreak. His documents reference "adjusted taxation" and "diverted cargo" — the same patterns Eric was tracking.

Whether the convoy diversion and the artifact modification were carried out by the same actor is an open question that points outward from Hollowgate.

Cavehopper
Small Monstrosity · Unaligned
Cavehoppers are twisted things bred for bloodsport and kept as status pets by kobolds. They are disarmingly cute — until they aren't. Lightning fast and merciless, they harass and tear at their foes with razor teeth and spiny quills.

Traits
Twitchbound Leap. The Cavehopper does not provoke opportunity attacks when it moves after making a Bite attack.
Actions

Quill Spit. Ranged Weapon Attack: +2 to hit, range 15 ft., one target. Hit: 1d2 piercing damage. The Cavehopper spits a sharp quill.
Combat Notes — Running the Cavehopper
  • The cute phase is not an act. The cavehopper genuinely investigates the party with curiosity — nose twitching, head cocked, chirping softly. This makes the trigger more shocking, not less. Let the party relax into it.
  • The trigger. Any sudden movement sets it off — standing, reaching, stepping forward, drawing a weapon, even an abrupt gesture. The GM decides the threshold, but err on the side of "surprisingly easy to trigger." The party should feel they were ambushed by their own instincts.
  • Use Twitchbound Leap aggressively once combat begins — Bite then immediately reposition, forcing the party to chase it or eat Quill Spit on the next turn.
  • It uses terrain: vaults onto wagons, scrambles up walls, springs from shadows. The chieftain's den is designed for it, not for the party.
  • When bloodied, it becomes erratic and relentless — increase its movement and prioritize the most injured target.
  • The chieftain watches the whole fight without intervening. Run him as genuinely uncertain whether this is a problem or entertainment.
  • Kobold bloodsport note: Kobolds raise cavehoppers from nests on a diet of insects, meat, and arena-bred excitement. A well-fed cavehopper is a kobold's pride and joy — and often the last thing their enemies see.

GM Summary — The Arc's Purpose

  • Start as a simple food shortage / bandit problem. Let it feel solvable. It isn't just a kobold problem.
  • The wagon food not being taken is the first real signal that something is wrong — reinforce it when the party finds the abandoned wagons.
  • Robyn is the emotional entry point into the kobold world. Treat him with care — he earns player sympathy quickly if played right.
  • The chieftain is comedy with weight. His absolute sincerity makes him memorable. He is not a villain — he just wanted to show off his pet.
  • The cavehopper trigger should feel like the party's fault, even when it isn't. That's the horror of it.
  • The kobold chase is embarrassed damage control, not aggression. Rocks and spears are thrown by kobolds who are mortified, not enraged.
  • Eric's body is the turning point. Everything after it is a different kind of story.
